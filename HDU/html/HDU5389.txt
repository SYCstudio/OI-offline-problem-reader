<tr><td align=center><h1 style='color:#1A5CC8'>Zero Escape</h1><font><b><span style='font-family:Arial;font-size:12px;font-weight:bold;color:green'>Time Limit: 2000/1000 MS (Java/Others)&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Memory Limit: 131072/131072 K (Java/Others)<br>Total Submission(s): 1705&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Accepted Submission(s): 871<br></span></b></font><br><br><div class=panel_title align=left>Problem Description</div> <div class=panel_content>Zero Escape, is a visual novel adventure video game directed by Kotaro Uchikoshi (you may hear about ever17?) and developed by Chunsoft.<br><br>Stilwell is enjoying the first chapter of this series, and in this chapter digital root is an important factor. <br><br>This is the definition of digital root on Wikipedia:<br>The digital root of a non-negative integer is the single digit value obtained by an iterative process of summing digits, on each iteration using the result from the previous iteration to compute a digit sum. The process continues until a single-digit number is reached.<br>For example, the digital root of $65536$ is $7$, because $6 + 5 + 5 + 3 + 6 = 25$ and $2 + 5 = 7$.<br><br>In the game, every player has a special identifier. Maybe two players have the same identifier, but they are different players. If a group of players want to get into a door numbered $X(1\leq X\leq 9)$, the digital root of their identifier sum must be $X$.<br>For example, players $\{1,2,6\}$ can get into the door $9$,  but players $\{2,3,3\}$ can't.<br><br>There is two doors, numbered $A$ and $B$. Maybe $A=B$, but they are two different door.<br>And there is $n$ players, everyone must get into one of these two doors. Some players will get into the door $A$, and others will get into the door $B$.<br>For example: <br>players are $\{1,2,6\}$, $A=9$, $B=1$<br>There is only one way to distribute the players: all players get into the door $9$. Because there is no player to get into the door $1$, the digital root limit of this door will be ignored.<br><br>Given the identifier of every player, please calculate how many kinds of methods are there, $mod~258280327$.</div><div class=panel_bottom>&nbsp;</div><br><div class=panel_title align=left>Input</div> <div class=panel_content>The first line of the input contains a single number $T$, the number of test cases.<br>For each test case, the first line contains three integers $n$, $A$ and $B$.<br>Next line contains $n$ integers $id_i$, describing the identifier of every player.<br>$T\leq 100$, $n\leq 10^5$, $\sum n\leq 10^6$, $1\leq A,B,id_i\leq 9$</div><div class=panel_bottom>&nbsp;</div><br><div class=panel_title align=left>Output</div> <div class=panel_content>For each test case, output a single integer in a single line, the number of ways that these $n$ players can get into these two doors.</div><div class=panel_bottom>&nbsp;</div><br><div class=panel_title align=left>Sample Input</div><div class=panel_content><pre><div style="font-family:Courier New,Courier,monospace;">4
3 9 1
1 2 6
3 9 1
2 3 3
5 2 3
1 1 1 1 1
9 9 9
1 2 3 4 5 6 7 8 9</div></pre></div><div class=panel_bottom>&nbsp;</div><br><div class=panel_title align=left>Sample Output</div><div class=panel_content><pre><div style="font-family:Courier New,Courier,monospace;">1
0
10
